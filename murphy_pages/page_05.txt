3. Results 
3.1. Performance in test set 
Results in the test set using the best performing spatial foot- 
print in the noisy test set for each inversion are summarized in 
Fig. 2 . In the noise-free case, all three inversion estimates corre- 
late with the true stiffness, with ILI showing the strongest correla- 
tion ( R = 0.979, R = 0.821, and R = 0.728 for ILI, HLI, and DI, re- 
spectively). This result shows that ILI improves estimation accuracy 
of spatially varying stiffness. ILI ( R = 0.940) continues to provide 
higher correlation than HLI ( R = 0.798) and DI ( R = 0.707) with 
noise added. Results using all tested spatial footprints are shown 
in Table 1 . 
3.2. CHO simulations 
Results from the CHO experiments using all tested spatial foot- 
prints for all three inversions are summarized in Table 2 . Brieﬂy, 
ILI with a 9 × 9 × 9 spatial footprint provided more accurate es- 
timates of inclusion stiffness in noisy data than DI or HLI of any 
tested spatial footprint. With the smallest spatial footprint tested 
(5 × 5 × 5 voxels), HLI provided the most accurate stiffness es- 
timates in the high noise homogeneous dataset. In low noise and 
noise-free datasets, DI provided the most accurate stiffness esti- 
mates in homogeneous data. For 5 × 5 × 5 and 7 × 7 × 7 voxel 
footprints, the learned inversions provide lower variance stiffness 
estimates in noisy homogeneous simulations than DI. Fig. 3 allows 
visualization of the partial volume effect induced by the homo- 
geneity assumption in HLI and DI with large footprints. As inclu- 
sion size decreases, HLI and DI stiffness estimates move closer to 
the no contrast line. This effect is mitigated by ILI. 
3.3. Phantom experiment 
Phantom inversions of the 7 percent driver power scan are 
shown in Fig. 4 . ILI (9 × 9 × 9 spatial footprint) more clearly delin- 
eates the 1.75 cm, 2 cm, and 2.25 cm diameter inclusions than HLI 
(9 × 9 × 9 spatial footprint), and inclusions < 2.5 cm in diameter 
are diﬃcult to detect with DI (11 × 11 × 11 spatial footprint). ILI 
also shows a higher CNR than HLI and DI for all inclusions at all 
driver power levels ( Fig. 5 ). The likelihood ratio test showed that 
the linear mixed effects model including inversion as a ﬁxed ef- 
fect signiﬁcantly improved prediction of CNR ( p = 0.001). Because 
the phantom data has high SNR, the large spatial footprints that 
provided optimal performance in the noisy test set are unneces- 
sary. A footprint of 5 × 5 × 5 voxels optimized the CNR for both 
HLI and DI in the phantom. Inversion results with these footprints 
more clearly depict the inclusions than the large footprint results 
(Supplemental Fig. 1), but still provide lower CNR in all inclusions 
at all driver power levels than ILI with a 9 × 9 × 9 footprint (Sup- 
plemental Fig. 2, p < 0.01, likelihood ratio test). ILI also shows a 
sharper transition at the inclusion boundaries and more accurate 
stiffness estimates than HLI and DI with large footprints ( Fig. 6 ) or 
the optimal footprints for the phantom data (Supplemental Fig. 3). 
Including inversion as a ﬁxed effect signiﬁcantly improved the pre- 
diction of errors in stiffness estimates in both cases ( p < 0.001 for 
both).
